Reading the data

Severity is shaped by who has done the coding

In this dataset, Critical severity can only originate from EuRepoC or a small hand-curated layer. The VERIS Community Database caps at High, and ransomware leak-site claims are coded Medium unless the victim was widely reported.

EuRepoC's fully expert-reviewed release covers incidents up to 31 December 2024. Anything after that date comes from their live TableView working data, which EuRepoC state is not fully expert-reviewed — and most of those records are still marked as being coded rather than finished. Recent months are also dominated by ransomware leak-site claims.

So any apparent change in Critical and High severity after 2024 reflects coding lag, coding status and source composition. It should not be read as a change in real-world severity.

The Where the data comes from, by year chart shows this composition directly, and every incident card states which layer and coding status it came from.

Six other things worth knowing

  • These are documented incidents, not all attacks. The overwhelming majority are never publicly disclosed or catalogued.
  • Growth partly reflects better recording. Disclosure regimes, breach notification laws and dataset coverage all expanded over this period. Some of the rise is real; some is improved measurement. The two cannot be cleanly separated.
  • Coverage is uneven by geography. Countries with mandatory disclosure and active research communities appear far more often. A sparse region means sparse reporting, not safety.
  • Attacker origins are usually proxies. Where a country is shown as an origin it generally reflects public attribution, an indictment, sanctions or an ecosystem association — not a verified launch location.
  • Leak-site claims are unverified. They are the attacker's own account, and some claimed victims dispute them.
  • Vulnerability counts are context, not attacks. The CVE and KEV series measure attack surface and known exploitation, not incidents.

About this map

Mapped incident sources: a ~90-incident curated layer embedded in this file (works fully offline), plus — when hosted — EuRepoC (2000–2024), VCDB, and recent-months ransomware leak-site claims from Ransomware.live, deduplicated across sources. Incident actors link to a cited threat-group directory (MITRE ATT&CK + Ransomware.live profiles). MITRE ATT&CK and CISA KEV load as enrichment. Origins are attribution or ecosystem proxies — see each incident's note.

Limitations

This map shows documented incidents, not all attacks. Growth over time reflects both a real rise in activity and substantially improved recording — the two cannot be cleanly separated. Sparse regions indicate sparse reporting, not safety. Attacker origins are usually attribution or ecosystem proxies rather than verified launch locations, and leak-site entries are unverified attacker claims.

Methodology

Records are normalised to a common incident schema and deduplicated across sources using date, victim country and a suffix-normalised victim-name key. Ransomware.live records are leak-site claims — attacker assertions, placed at country level, with no origin asserted. Unknown attacker locations launch from a neutral ocean anchor. Incident actors are matched against a threat-group directory built from an embedded core plus MITRE ATT&CK intrusion sets and Ransomware.live group profiles, each cited with links.
